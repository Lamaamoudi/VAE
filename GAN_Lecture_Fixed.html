1 · Motivation — Why GANs?
The problem with traditional generative models
▾

Before GANs, generative models learned to model data by explicitly estimating the probability density of the data. Three major families existed:

1. Autoregressive Models

FACTORED DISTRIBUTION
pθ(x)=∏i=1Npθ(xi∣x<i)

Generates one pixel/token at a time — slow.

2. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

MARGINAL LIKELIHOOD
pθ(x)=∫zpθ(x∣z)pθ(z)dz

Blurry outputs due to the ELBO approximation.

3. Normalizing Flow Models

CHANGE OF VARIABLES
pX(x;θ)=pZ(fθ−1(x))|det∂fθ−1(x)∂x|

Requires invertible architectures — very constrained.

⚠️ The Core Problems
  • Complex approximations or MCMC sampling methods needed
  • Often produce blurry or unrealistic samples
  • Explicit density estimation is computationally expensive
💡 The Key Insight

"What if we give up on explicitly modeling density, and just want the ability to sample?"

We don't need a probability formula — we just need to produce realistic data. This is the spark that led to GANs.

🎭 Real-World Analogy: The Art Forger & The Detective

Think of a art forger (Generator) trying to paint fake Picassos, and a art detective (Discriminator) trying to catch fakes. As the forger gets better at fooling the detective, and the detective gets sharper at spotting fakes — both improve each other. Eventually the forger produces paintings indistinguishable from real art. That's a GAN!

2 · Definition & Core Components
Two networks, one adversarial game
▾
📌 Introduced by Ian Goodfellow et al. (2014)

GANs do not require explicit density estimation or Markov chains. They are a likelihood-free approach to generative modeling.

The Two-Sample Test Idea

Given a finite set of samples from two distributions S1={x~P} and S2={x~Q}, how can we tell if P=Q?

We use a test statistic T that compares S1 and S2 — e.g., difference in means, variances. Crucially, T is likelihood-free: it only uses samples, never the density functions P or Q.

  • Set S1=𝒟={x~pdata} (real data) and S2={x~pθ} (model samples)
  • Train the Generator to minimize the two-sample test objective between S1 and S2
  • But how do we get a two-sample test objective? Train another network — the Discriminator — to discriminate between the two sets
  • And voilà! That's a GAN.

🔵 Generator G

Takes random noise vector z (latent vector) and maps it to a fake data sample G(z).

Goal: Produce samples that fool D into thinking they are real.

Input: noise z Output: fake data

🔴 Discriminator D

Receives either real data or generated fake data and outputs a probability — real (1) or fake (0).

Goal: Correctly classify real vs. fake samples.

Input: image Output: P(real)

The approach is adversarial since the two networks have opposing objectives. They play a two-player zero-sum game.

3 · Training GANs
The minimax game
▾

The Minimax Objective

Both networks are trained jointly in a min-max game:

GAN MINIMAX OBJECTIVE
minθGmaxθD[𝔼x~pdatalogDθD(x)+𝔼z~p(z)log(1−DθD(GθG(z)))]
📈 Discriminator θ_D wants to MAXIMIZE
  • D(x)→1 for real data x
  • D(G(z))→0 for fake data G(z)

So logD(x) is large and log(1−D(G(z))) is large.

📉 Generator θ_G wants to MINIMIZE
  • D(G(z))→1 (fool the discriminator!)

So log(1−D(G(z))) becomes very negative — the term it controls.

Training Algorithm

  • Train the Discriminator: Sample real data x~pdata and noise z~p(z). Update θD to maximize the objective.
  • Train the Generator: Sample noise z~p(z). Update θG to minimize the objective (keep θD fixed).
  • Repeat: Alternate these steps until convergence (Nash equilibrium).
💡 Training Tips (from the lecture)
  • ✅ Use Batch Normalization
  • ✅ Use Leaky ReLU in the discriminator
  • ✅ Avoid sigmoid output in the discriminator
🔥 Practical Issues
  • Discriminator can become too strong — crushing the generator
  • Vanishing gradients for the generator when discriminator is perfect
  • Careful balance of training speeds is necessary
  • See Soumith Chintala's "How to Train a GAN?" for practical hacks

4 · Understanding the Objective Function
Nash equilibrium, JS divergence, and the optimal discriminator
▾
🎯 Goal

Find a Nash Equilibrium between the generator and discriminator — a point where neither can improve unilaterally. The generator produces samples indistinguishable from real data.

The Optimal Discriminator

For a fixed generator G, the optimal discriminator D* is:

OPTIMAL DISCRIMINATOR
D*(x)=pdata(x)pdata(x)+pG(x)

When pG=pdata (perfect generator), D*(x)=12 everywhere — the discriminator is completely confused (50/50 guess). This is the Nash equilibrium.

Connection to JS Divergence

Plugging D* back into the minimax objective, the minimum over generators becomes:

MINIMUM GENERATOR LOSS
minGV(G,D*)=−log4+2·JSD(pdata‖pG)

where JSD is the Jensen-Shannon Divergence. This is minimized when pG=pdata, confirming the GAN objective is theoretically sound.

⚠️ Problem: JSD saturates!

When the two distributions are very different (far apart), JSD saturates at log2 — giving zero gradient. The generator can't learn. This is the vanishing gradient problem (discussed next).

6 · Problems with GANs
Vanishing gradients, Nash failure, and mode collapse
▾

Problem 1 — Vanishing Gradients

When the discriminator becomes too good, it achieves:

PERFECT DISCRIMINATOR
D(x)=1∀x∈pdata,D(x)=0∀x∈pG

The loss drops to zero — no gradient flows to the generator. Training stalls.

✅ Solution: Non-Saturating Generator Loss

Instead of minimizing log(1−D(G(z))), the generator performs gradient ascent on:

ALTERNATIVE GENERATOR OBJECTIVE
maxθG𝔼z~p(z)logDθD(GθG(z))

This provides stronger gradients early in training when D(G(z))≈0.

Problem 2 — Difficulty Achieving Nash Equilibrium

GANs involve training two models simultaneously in a two-player non-cooperative game. Each model updates its cost independently. Because each model's loss depends on the other's parameters, convergence is not guaranteed — the models can oscillate or diverge.

📖 Resource

soumith/ganhacks — "How to Train a GAN? Tips and tricks" by Soumith Chintala

Problem 3 — Mode Collapse

🎯 What is Mode Collapse?

A data distribution often has multiple modes (clusters). E.g., MNIST has 10 modes (digits 0–9). If G finds one output that always fools D, it may repeatedly produce only that one output — ignoring all other modes. The generator "collapses" to a single output.

On MNIST, this means the generator produces only one digit (e.g., only "4"s) even though it should produce all 10.

🔧 Solutions to Mode Collapse

Solutions are mostly empirical:

  • Alternative architectures (e.g., progressive growing)
  • Modified GAN losses (e.g., Wasserstein distance)
  • Additional regularization terms (e.g., minibatch discrimination)

8 · Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)
Earth Mover's Distance — a smoother loss landscape
▾
📄 Paper: Arjovsky et al., "Wasserstein GAN," 2017 — arXiv:1701.07875

The Core Idea

WGAN replaces the original cross-entropy / JSD-based loss with the Wasserstein distance (also called Earth Mover's Distance, or EMD). This provides:

  • A smoother gradient everywhere — even when distributions don't overlap
  • A meaningful loss that correlates with sample quality
  • More stable training with fewer hacks needed

Wasserstein Distance — The Intuition

⛏️ The "Earth Moving" Analogy

Imagine two probability distributions as piles of dirt. The Wasserstein distance is the minimum amount of work (dirt × distance moved) required to reshape one pile into the other. A step-by-step plan is computed to move dirt between piles P and Q to make them match.

Unlike KL or JS divergence, this measure is continuous and differentiable even when the distributions don't overlap at all — solving the vanishing gradient problem!

WGAN Objective

WASSERSTEIN GAN OBJECTIVE (DUAL FORM)
minGmaxD∈ℱ𝔼x~pdata[D(x)]−𝔼z~p(z)[D(G(z))]

where ℱ is the set of 1-Lipschitz functions. The discriminator in WGAN is called a "critic" (no sigmoid — outputs real values, not probabilities).

Key Changes from Original GAN

PropertyOriginal GANWGAN
Loss functionCross-entropy (log loss)Wasserstein distance
Discriminator outputSigmoid (probability)Linear (real-valued critic)
Gradient signalSaturates when D is too goodSmooth gradients always
Lipschitz constraintNoneWeight clipping or gradient penalty
Loss vs qualityNo correlationCorrelates with sample quality
✅ Result

WGAN produces higher quality bedroom images and more stable training curves. The loss value is actually interpretable — lower loss = better samples.

9 · Conditional GAN (cGAN)
Controlled generation with labels and conditions
▾

Core Idea

The original GAN has no control over what it generates. Conditional GANs fix this by feeding a condition y (e.g., class label, another image) to both the generator and discriminator.

Noise z
+
Label y
→
Generator G(z|y)
→
Fake G(z|y)
↘
Discriminator D(x|y)

cGAN Losses

DISCRIMINATOR LOSS
ℒ(D)=−𝔼x~pdata[logD(x∣y)]−𝔼z~pz(z),y~pdata(y)[log(1−D(G(z∣y)))]
GENERATOR LOSS
ℒ(G)=−𝔼z[logD(G(z∣y))]

Image-to-Image Translation (pix2pix)

A powerful extension: the condition y is an entire image — e.g., a sketch, semantic map, or edge map. The generator translates from one domain to another.

🖼️ Adding L1 Loss

For image-to-image translation, an L1 loss is added alongside the GAN loss to encourage the generated image to be pixel-close to the target. This helps avoid blurriness while keeping fine details sharp via the adversarial loss.

COMBINED LOSS
ℒtotal=ℒGAN+λ·ℒL1

Applications

  • Sketch → Realistic photo
  • Semantic map → Street scene
  • Day → Night conversion
  • Greyscale → Color
  • MNIST: generate specific digits (0–9)

Advantage over GAN

Full control over the output. Instead of random generation, you can specify exactly what you want. Useful for data augmentation, creative tools, and domain adaptation.

10 · CycleGAN
Unpaired image-to-image translation
▾
📄 Paper: Zhu et al., "Unpaired Image-to-Image Translation using Cycle-Consistent Adversarial Networks," ICCV 2017 — arXiv:1703.10593

The Problem pix2pix Can't Solve

pix2pix requires paired training data — each input image must have a corresponding output image. This is expensive or impossible to collect (e.g., horse ↔ zebra pairs).

🐴🦓 The Horse-Zebra Problem

CycleGAN can learn to translate horses into zebras and back — without ever seeing a paired (horse, zebra) example. It uses only two unpaired datasets: a set of horse photos and a set of zebra photos.

Cycle Consistency: The Key Innovation

CycleGAN enforces that the translation is invertible. If you translate a horse to a zebra, then translate that zebra back to a horse, you should get the original horse back.

CYCLE: HORSE → ZEBRA → HORSE
horse→Ghorse→zebrazebra^→[Dzebra,Gzebra→horse]horse^
CYCLE: ZEBRA → HORSE → ZEBRA
zebra→Gzebra→horsehorse^→[Dhorse,Ghorse→zebra]zebra^

Architecture

CycleGAN has two generators and two discriminators:

Generators

  • GA→B: translates domain A → B
  • GB→A: translates domain B → A

Discriminators

  • DA: real vs fake in domain A
  • DB: real vs fake in domain B

Full Loss

CYCLEGAN TOTAL LOSS
ℒtotal=ℒGAN(GA→B,DB)+ℒGAN(GB→A,DA)+λℒcyc(GA→B,GB→A)

where the cycle consistency loss is:

CYCLE CONSISTENCY LOSS
ℒcyc=𝔼x~pA[‖GB→A(GA→B(x))−x‖1]+𝔼y~pB[‖GA→B(GB→A(y))−y‖1]

Applications

🐴→🦓

Horse ↔ Zebra

🍎→🍊

Apple ↔ Orange

🌅→🌃

Summer ↔ Winter

🖼️→🎨

Photo ↔ Painting

11 · StyleGAN
NVIDIA's style-based generator for photorealistic faces
▾
📄 Paper: Karras et al., "A Style-Based Generator Architecture for GANs," CVPR 2019 — arXiv:1812.04948

Goal

Better disentanglement of features in latent space. Control coarse features (pose, face shape) independently from fine features (hair texture, freckles) — all through the style vector.

Architecture Innovation

Instead of directly feeding noise z into the generator, StyleGAN uses a mapping network to map z∈𝒵 to a style vector w∈𝒲:

MAPPING NETWORK
z∈𝒵→fw∈𝒲

The style vector w is then injected at each layer of the synthesis network via Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN):

ADAPTIVE INSTANCE NORMALIZATION (AdaIN)
AdaIN(xi,y)=ys,i·xi−μ(xi)σ(xi)+yb,i

where y=(ys,yb) are the scale and bias predicted by a learned affine transformation A from the style vector w. This is extremely effective at controlling visual styles.

Block A and Block B

Block A — Style

Learned affine transformation that takes w and produces the AdaIN parameters (ys,yb). Controls coarse-to-fine style at each resolution.

Block B — Noise

Learned per-channel scaling factor applied to injected noise. Controls stochastic variation (hair strand positions, pores, etc.) — small random details.

Latent Space Choices

Which latent space to use for embedding and editing?

SpaceDimensionBest ForQuality
𝒵512-d—❌ Not good
𝒲512-dEditing real images✓ Better
𝒲+18 × 512Reconstruction / embedding✅ Best
💡 Why W is better than Z

The 𝒵 space is forced to be Gaussian, which entangles features. The mapping network f creates 𝒲, which is less entangled — moving in 𝒲 changes one attribute (e.g., age) without affecting others (e.g., gender). This is called disentanglement.

💡 W+ for Real Image Embedding

𝒲+ uses a different w vector for each of the 18 layers. This greatly increases expressiveness for embedding and reconstructing real images (at the cost of editability).

12 · Latent Space Interpolation
Navigating the space of learned concepts
▾

One of the most fascinating properties of GANs is that their latent space is meaningful and structured. Points that are close together in latent space correspond to similar images.

Interpolation

By linearly interpolating between two latent vectors z1 and z2:

LATENT INTERPOLATION
zt=(1−t)z1+tz2,t∈[0,1]

The generated images G(zt) smoothly morph from one face/object to another, passing through meaningful intermediate concepts.

t = 0.50
