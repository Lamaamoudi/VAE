Section 1 · Slides 4

GANs: Recap

⚡ Why Start Here?
Diffusion models exist because GANs and VAEs had fundamental problems. Understanding those problems is the motivation for everything that follows.

What Are GANs?

  • Implicit modeling: GANs don't model the data distribution directly — they learn it implicitly through competition.
  • Two-network adversarial game: The Generator creates fake samples to fool the Discriminator, while the discriminator tries to distinguish real from fake.
  • Former state-of-the-art: GANs dominated image generation due to their high visual quality outputs.

🚨 GAN Drawbacks

  • Training instability — the generator and discriminator easily get out of sync and diverge.
  • Mode collapse — the generator learns to produce only a few types of samples, ignoring the rest of the distribution.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity — small changes in learning rate or architecture can collapse training.

🌫️ VAE Drawbacks

  • Blurry outputs — VAEs optimize a lower bound and tend to produce over-smoothed, averaged-looking images.
  • Limited expressiveness — the Gaussian assumption on the posterior limits the model's ability to capture complex data distributions.
💡 The Core Challenge
Achieving both high sample quality AND stable, reliable training — this is the key unsolved tension that motivated diffusion models.

  • State-of-the-art sample quality & diversity
  • Probabilistic and interpretable framework

Introduction to Diffusion Models

📖 Definition
Diffusion models are a class of probabilistic generative models that learn to reverse a gradual noising process applied to data. They are inspired by concepts from thermodynamics and stochastic processes.

➡️ Forward Process

Progressively corrupt data by adding Gaussian noise, step by step, until only pure noise remains. This is predefined — not learned.

⬅️ Reverse Process

Learn to recover the original data by reversing the noising process. A neural network is trained to do this — this is what's learned.

🎨 Interactive: Forward & Reverse Process

Watch noise being added (forward) and removed (reverse) step by step.

🌡️ Thermodynamics Analogy
Imagine an ice cube melting into water — that's adding entropy/noise. Diffusion models learn to reverse the melting: starting from disordered noise, they reconstruct structured, realistic data.
Section 4 · Slides 9–11

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models

🏆 Milestone Paper
DDPM (Ho, Jain & Abbeel, NeurIPS 2020) — also known as score-based generative models — demonstrated that diffusion models can surpass GANs on image synthesis quality.

Fixed Forward Process $q$

Gradually adds Gaussian noise to an image until only pure noise remains. Predefined mathematically — not learned. Free to compute!

Learned Reverse Process $p_\theta$

A neural network is trained to gradually denoise an image starting from pure noise, recovering the original. This is the learned component.

💡 Elegant Design Choice
The forward process is free — we define it mathematically with simple Gaussians. The neural network only needs to learn the reverse, which turns out to be much simpler: just predict the noise!

📊 Historical Breakthrough

In 2021, Dhariwal & Nichol (OpenAI) published "Diffusion Models Beat GANs on Image Synthesis", showing that with the right architecture and guidance, diffusion models achieve better FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) scores than state-of-the-art GANs — a landmark result in generative modeling.

Key Variables

  • $x_0$ — the original clean data (image)
  • $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T$ — progressively noisier versions
  • $x_T$ — approximately pure Gaussian noise
  • $T$ — total number of timesteps (typically 1000)
  • $\beta_t$ — noise level added at step $t$ (the noise schedule)
Section 5 · Slides 12–14

Forward Diffusion Process

📐 Core Idea
The forward process is a Markov chain — each step depends only on the previous one. Over ~1000 steps, any image becomes indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise.

One Step: Adding Noise

Given $x_{t-1}$, we generate $x_t$ by sampling from a Gaussian:

Forward Transition Kernel
$$q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_t;\; \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\, x_{t-1},\; \beta_t\, \mathbf{I}\right)$$

Breaking Down the Equation

  • $\beta_t \in (0,1)$ — noise schedule value at step $t$
  • Mean $= \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\, x_{t-1}$ — the signal is shrunk slightly
  • Variance $= \beta_t\,\mathbf{I}$ — a proportional amount of noise is added
  • Sampling: $x_t = \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\, x_{t-1} + \sqrt{\beta_t}\, \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})$
Full Forward Process (Joint Distribution)
$$q(x_{1:T} \mid x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} q(x_t \mid x_{t-1})$$
🚀 Key Training Trick — Jump Directly to Any Timestep!
Define $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}(1 - \beta_s)$. Then we can sample $x_t$ from $x_0$ in one shot, skipping all intermediate steps:
Closed-Form Sampling from $x_0$ to $x_t$
$$q(x_t \mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_t;\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0,\; (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\,\mathbf{I}\right)$$

What $\bar\alpha_t$ Represents

  • When $t$ is small: $\bar\alpha_t \approx 1$ → image mostly clean
  • When $t$ is large: $\bar\alpha_t \approx 0$ → image is mostly noise
  • This lets us sample any noisy level during training instantly!
Section 6 · Slide 15

Noise Schedules

🎛️ What Is a Noise Schedule?
The noise schedule $\{\beta_t\}_{t=1}^T$ controls how much noise is added at each timestep. Its design critically affects training stability and sample quality.

📈 Schedule Comparison Chart

■ Linear β_t ■ Cosine β_t - - ᾱ_t (cosine)

📏 Linear Schedule

$\beta_t$ increases linearly from ~$10^{-4}$ to ~$0.02$.

  • Simple and intuitive
  • Can destroy signal too quickly at early timesteps
  • Used in the original DDPM paper

📐 Cosine Schedule

$\bar\alpha_t$ follows a cosine curve; $\beta_t$ is derived from it.

  • Preserves perceptual signal longer
  • More gradual noise at early steps
  • Proposed in Improved DDPM (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021)
  • Often yields better image generation
💡 Intuition
With a linear schedule, the image becomes unrecognizable very early on. The cosine schedule delays this, giving the model more "meaningful" timesteps to learn from.
Section 7 · Slides 16–18

Reverse Denoising Process

🎯 Goal
Start from pure Gaussian noise $p(x_T) = \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$ and iteratively denoise to recover a realistic image $x_0$.

The Problem: Reverse Is Intractable

At each step $t$, we want to sample $x_{t-1}$ from $x_t$. The true reverse $q(x_{t-1} \mid x_t)$ involves the entire data distribution — it's intractable. We need a neural network to approximate it.

Why Gaussian Approximation Is Valid

If each $\beta_t$ is small enough, the reverse step is also approximately Gaussian — this is a well-known result from diffusion theory. So we model:

Learned Reverse Step (General)
$$p_\theta(x_{t-1} \mid x_t) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_{t-1};\; \mu_\theta(x_t, t),\; \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)\right)$$
⚙️ Simplification Used in DDPM
Fix the variance to $\beta_t \mathbf{I}$ and only learn the mean $\mu_\theta$. This works well in practice and simplifies the objective:
Simplified Reverse Step (Fixed Variance)
$$p_\theta(x_{t-1} \mid x_t) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_{t-1};\; \mu_\theta(x_t, t),\; \beta_t\,\mathbf{I}\right)$$
Full Reverse Joint Distribution
$$p_\theta(x_{0:T}) = p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_{t-1} \mid x_t)$$
🧠 Intuition
The neural network $\mu_\theta$ is like a "denoising oracle" — given a noisy image $x_t$ at noise level $t$, it predicts what the slightly cleaner version $x_{t-1}$ should look like.
Section 8 · Slides 19–20

ELBO & Variational Inference

❓ The Problem
Directly maximizing the marginal likelihood $p(x)$ is intractable — we can't compute the integral over all latent variables. We need a workaround!

The Solution: Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)

Introduce an approximate posterior $q(z \mid x)$ and use Jensen's inequality to get a tractable lower bound on $\log p(x)$:

ELBO as Lower Bound on Log-Likelihood
$$\log p(x) \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_{q(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{p(x,z)}{q(z \mid x)}\right] = \text{ELBO}$$

🔄 Reconstruction Term

Measures how well the model can reconstruct $x$ from latent variables. Pushes the model toward accuracy.

📏 KL Divergence Term

Penalizes divergence between approximate posterior $q(z|x)$ and prior $p(z)$. Acts as regularization.

🎓 Why Maximize ELBO?
Maximizing ELBO simultaneously: (1) makes the model reconstruct data well, and (2) keeps latent distributions close to the prior. When the bound is tight (gap → 0), we recover the true log-likelihood.

ELBO in Diffusion Models

Applying variational inference to the diffusion framework, the upper bound on negative log-likelihood becomes:

Variational Upper Bound
$$\mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)}\!\left[-\log p_\theta(x_0)\right] \;\leq\; \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)q(x_{1:T}|x_0)}\!\left[-\log \frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T} \mid x_0)}\right]$$
Decomposed as Sum of Per-Timestep Losses
$$L = L_0 + L_1 + \cdots + L_T$$

Learning Denoising Models

💡 The Big Reparametrization Trick
Instead of predicting the mean $\mu_\theta$ directly, we reparametrize so the network predicts the added noise $\epsilon_\theta$. Simpler, more stable, and works better!

Mean as a Function of Predicted Noise

The neural network $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$ predicts the noise. The mean can then be computed as:

Mean Reparametrization
$$\mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\!\left(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\right)$$

Variable Reference

  • $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$ — signal retention at step $t$
  • $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1-\beta_s)$ — cumulative signal retention up to step $t$
  • $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$ — the neural network acting as a noise predictor

🎯 Final Training Objective (Simplified)

For randomly sampled $t$ and noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})$:

$\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}}$ — The Training Loss
$$\mathcal{L}_t = \left\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta\!\left(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon,\; t\right)\right\|^2$$
🤯 Reading the Loss Step-by-Step
  1. Sample a clean image $x_0$ and noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})$
  2. Mix: $x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t} x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon$ (closed-form noising)
  3. Feed $x_t$ and timestep $t$ into the neural network
  4. Compare network output to the actual noise $\epsilon$ via MSE
  5. Backpropagate and optimize. That's it!
🔗 Connection to Hierarchical VAEs
Diffusion models are a special form of hierarchical VAEs, where: the encoder is fixed (forward process), latent variables have the same dimension as the data, the denoising model is shared across all timesteps, and training uses a reweighted variational bound.
Section 10 · Slides 22–23

Training & Sampling Algorithms

Algorithm 1 · Training
1repeat
2  $x_0 \sim q(x_0)$ // sample clean data
3  $t \sim \text{Uniform}(1, T)$ // random step
4  $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$ // sample noise
5  corrupt: $x_t \leftarrow \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t} x_0 + \sqrt{1{-}\bar\alpha_t}\epsilon$
6  predict: $\hat\epsilon = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$
7  loss: $\mathcal{L} = \|\epsilon - \hat\epsilon\|^2$
8  optimize with Adam / SGD
9until converged
Algorithm 2 · Sampling
1$x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$ // start: pure noise
2for $t = T, T{-}1, \ldots, 1$ do
3  predict: $\hat\epsilon = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$
4  compute: $\mu_\theta(x_t, t)$
5  $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})$ if $t{>}1$, else $z{=}0$
6  $x_{t-1} = \mu_\theta(x_t,t) + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,z$
7  // optionally apply guidance
8end for
9return $x_0$
⏱️ Cost of Sampling
Generating one image requires $T$ forward passes through the network (e.g. 1000 steps = 1000 neural network evaluations). This is the primary bottleneck — GANs only need 1!

Optional: Classifier-Free Guidance

During sampling (step 7), we can apply classifier-free guidance to steer generation toward a desired condition (e.g., a text prompt). The network is trained both with and without conditioning, then guidance is applied as a weighted interpolation at inference time. This dramatically improves sample quality for conditional generation.

Section 11 · Slide 24

Network Architectures

🏗️ Standard Architecture
Diffusion models use U-Net architectures with ResNet blocks and self-attention layers to represent $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$. Time conditioning is essential — the network must know what noise level it's at.

U-Net Noise Predictor $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$

🧱 Key Components

  • ResNet blocks — stable deep feature extraction
  • Self-attention layers — captures global image context
  • Skip connections — preserve spatial detail between encoder and decoder
  • Downsampling / Upsampling — hierarchical feature scales

⏰ Time Conditioning

The network must know which noise level it's at! Timestep $t$ is encoded as a vector and injected into each ResNet block via learned scale and shift (FiLM conditioning).

  • Sinusoidal positional embeddings — like in Transformers
  • Random Fourier features — alternative approach
Section 12 · Slides 26–29

Issues & Research Directions

⚠️ Main Limitation: Slow Sampling
Diffusion models require hundreds to thousands of iterative steps to generate one sample. A single image can take seconds to minutes — far slower than GANs (1 forward pass).

🔬 Research Directions to Address These Issues

  • DDIM (Non-Markovian Sampling): Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models skip timesteps with a deterministic process. Reduces 1000 steps to ~50 with similar quality.
  • Improved DDPM: Learn the noise variance $\sigma^2$ during training rather than fixing it — better flexibility and performance.
  • Score-Based SDEs: Model the score function (gradient of log-density) using stochastic differential equations — unified framework for all diffusion variants.
  • Denoising Diffusion GANs: Use a GAN to model each denoising step — combines speed of GANs with quality/diversity of diffusion.
  • Cascaded Diffusion: Chain multiple diffusion models at increasing resolutions (e.g., 64→256→1024) — boosts fidelity at high resolution (used in Imagen).

🎯 Everything You Learned

  • GANs vs Diffusion: GANs are fast but unstable; diffusion is stable with better quality but slow sampling.
  • Forward Process: Fixed Markov chain adding Gaussian noise. Closed-form $q(x_t|x_0)$ via $\bar\alpha_t$ enables efficient training.
  • Noise Schedules: Linear is simple; cosine preserves signal longer at early steps.
  • Reverse Process: Learned Gaussian chain. Network predicts noise $\epsilon_\theta$, not mean directly (key reparametrization).
  • ELBO: Variational lower bound — reconstruction + KL regularization, decomposed per timestep.
  • Loss: $\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\epsilon,\, t)\|^2$ — simply predict the noise!
  • Architecture: U-Net + ResNet blocks + self-attention + sinusoidal time embeddings.
  • Connection to VAEs: Hierarchical VAE with fixed encoder, same-dim latents, shared decoder.
  • Issues: Slow sampling — the generative trilemma (quality vs. diversity vs. speed).
  • Solutions: DDIM (skip steps), Improved DDPM (learned variance), score SDEs, diffusion-GAN hybrid.
  • Applications: GLIDE, DALL·E 2, Imagen, Diffusion Autoencoders, Super-Resolution, 3D Generation.
